Wounds & Cuts

Basic Wound Care

  • Rinse the wound with clean running water for at least 1‚Äì2 minutes ‚Äî do not pour alcohol directly into an open wound as it damages tissue
  • Gently clean around the wound with soap and water, avoiding the wound itself
  • Apply gentle pressure with a clean cloth to stop bleeding
  • Cover with a clean bandage or dressing; change the dressing daily or whenever it gets wet or dirty
  • If available, apply a thin layer of antibiotic ointment before covering

Signs of Infection ‚Äî Seek Medical Help

  • Increasing redness, warmth, or swelling around the wound
  • Pus or cloudy discharge
  • Red streaks spreading from the wound
  • Fever (temperature above 38¬∞C / 100.4¬∞F)
  • Wound that is not improving after 2‚Äì3 days
After a storm, all water may be contaminated

Use boiled or bottled water to rinse wounds if tap water safety has not been confirmed. Follow the official Boil Water Advisory if one is in effect.

Carbon Monoxide Poisoning

üö´ NEVER run a generator, grill, camp stove, or any fuel-burning device indoors ‚Äî including garages, even with the door open.

Carbon monoxide (CO) is colourless and odourless. You cannot see or smell it. It can kill within minutes.

Symptoms ‚Äî "Flu-like but No Fever"

  • Headache (often the first sign)
  • Dizziness or lightheadedness
  • Weakness and fatigue
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Shortness of breath
  • Chest pain
  • Confusion or altered mental state (severe ‚Äî call 919)

Key difference from flu: CO poisoning has no fever, and symptoms improve when you go outside and get fresh air.

If CO Poisoning Is Suspected

  • Get everyone out of the building immediately ‚Äî do not stop to collect belongings
  • Leave the door open as you exit
  • Do not go back inside for any reason
  • Call 919 from outside or a neighbour's home
  • Move to fresh air and stay there until emergency services clear the building

Generator Safety Rules

  • Place the generator at least 20 feet (6 metres) away from all windows, doors, and vents
  • Point the exhaust away from the house
  • Never use a generator in a garage, carport, basement, or shed
  • Install battery-operated CO detectors in your home ‚Äî test them monthly

Dehydration

Signs of Dehydration

  • Dry mouth and increased thirst
  • Little or no urination, or very dark yellow urine
  • Dizziness or lightheadedness
  • Rapid heartbeat
  • Headache and fatigue
  • Dry, cool skin

Treatment

  • Drink small sips of clean water frequently ‚Äî do not gulp large amounts at once
  • If available, use Oral Rehydration Salts (ORS) ‚Äî these replace lost salt and minerals more effectively than plain water
  • Rest in a cool, shaded area and avoid exertion
  • Avoid caffeinated drinks and alcohol ‚Äî they worsen dehydration

Heat Exhaustion vs. Heat Stroke

Heat Exhaustion ‚Äî Treat, Monitor Closely

  • Signs: Heavy sweating, cool or pale skin, fast weak pulse, nausea, muscle cramps, dizziness, weakness
  • Move the person to a cool or shaded area immediately
  • Loosen tight clothing
  • Apply cool, wet cloths to skin; use a fan if available
  • Give cool water to sip if the person is conscious and able to swallow
  • Monitor closely ‚Äî if symptoms worsen or do not improve within 15 minutes, call 919

Heat Stroke ‚Äî EMERGENCY: Call 919

‚ö† Key rule: Confusion or loss of consciousness = Heat Stroke = Call 919 immediately
  • Signs: Hot, red, dry or damp skin ¬∑ Rapid, strong pulse ¬∑ Confusion, slurred speech, or unconsciousness ¬∑ Temperature above 39.5¬∞C (103¬∞F)
  • Call 919 first, then begin cooling immediately while waiting
  • Move the person to a cool area; remove excess clothing
  • Cool with whatever is available: cold water poured over the body, wet cloths, ice packs to neck, armpits, and groin
  • Do NOT give the person anything to drink if they are confused or unconscious

Medication Management During Power Outages

Prepare Before the Storm

  • Keep a written medication list in your go-bag at all times ‚Äî include the medication name, dose, and prescribing doctor's contact
  • Try to have at least a 30-day supply of all critical medications before hurricane season
  • Ask your doctor or pharmacist about an emergency supply before a storm is announced
  • Store medications in a waterproof, portable container in your go-bag

Insulin & Refrigerated Medications

  • Insulin should ideally be kept at 2‚Äì8¬∞C (36‚Äì46¬∞F) in a refrigerator
  • When power is out, keep insulin in a cooler with ice or ice packs ‚Äî wrap the insulin in a cloth so it does not touch ice directly (freezing damages insulin)
  • An unopened vial or pen of insulin can generally be kept at room temperature (below 25‚Äì30¬∞C) for up to 28 days, depending on the brand ‚Äî check your specific product's label
  • Insulin that has been frozen, overheated, or appears cloudy/discoloured (for normally clear types) should not be used
  • Ask your pharmacist before the storm about your specific insulin's room-temperature stability

During an Outage

  • Do not skip critical medications (insulin, blood pressure, anti-seizure, heart medications) without medical guidance
  • Contact a pharmacist, clinic, or hospital if your supply is running low
  • If you must evacuate, bring your full medication supply and written list in your go-bag
  • Many pharmacies can provide an emergency refill during a declared disaster ‚Äî ask
Keep a medication list in your go-bag
